Brief

This year, I was asked to create a website for the Wellington Speaking Union (WSU), an organisation that runs public speaking and debating tournaments for students.
Their old website was outdated, and Brooke, the current President of WSU wanted a redone site for their use.
Before creating the website, Brooke and I needed to collaborate and discuss what this website would be used for, and what we wanted to achieve out of its creation. From this point, we created a list of our key aims that would allow Brooke to be as involved in the process, and in providing feedback throughout this project.
In any website, it is important that UX studies are conducted so that the website fits the needs of not only the owner, but also the consumers who will be using and navigating the website.

  • I contacted and interviewed four categories of users who would be accessing and interacting with the website. I wanted to find out their frustrations with the old site, and their expectations for the design and the active elements of the site.
  • I also did competition analysis to start having an understanding of what needed to be included in this site and what other similar websites had used in terms of design and the features that they included. This also enables me to think about both what their site is lacking, and doing a good job in to help me when choosing what would work for this site.
  • Finally, completing a project backlog to have a better grasp on the "big picture" idea for this site but evidently, this is just a simple plan with none of the specific details of features and designs included as these are subject to change.

Relevant Implications (describe)

  1. Intellectual Property
    Intellectual property is the idea of who owns the images, ideas, and the text that is used on the website. This involves trademark and copyright law, whether the images used have been copyrighted, what rules apply to ensure that all of these products of intellect have been used legally. For example, images used should only be displayed if given permission by the person who owns them. The easiest way to do this is to take your own photos, but if using images off the internet, it is important to understand Creative Commons licensing. The idea is that all work is copyrighted even if the symbol is not used or it’s not clearly stated, but if the author has given permission for others to freely use (or use with restrictions) their work, then it is okay. This is extremely important because no one likes having their work stolen from them, or not having their work clearly depicted as being theirs.
    14
  2. Accessibility
    Accessibility means that all users who want to use the site, can. Generally, this is twofold: firstly, that anyone who could access the site, is able to (through any sort of device). Secondly, that all types of people are able to use it. There are a large variety of groups who may have disabilities that need recognition such as those with dyslexia, colour-blindness, impaired vision. However, these people also need to be able to successfully use the resources that are available on the website. By considering the implications of these, having a focus on accessibility means ensuring that there are ways / changes on the website which allow easy navigation and use of the website. For example, this could include having responsive design that adjusts the content on the page depending on the size of the browser. Or ensuring that the text is large enough for all to read, and that the colour scheme is appropriate (not using bright yellow against a white background).
    14
  3. Aesthetics
    Aesthetics focuses on making the website visually-appealing and appreciating the graphic design involved. This is quite important because someone’s first impression of a website may change their attitudes towards it. For example, if all of the photos are grainy, and the website is not formatted properly, someone may think that the information on it, or the resources are no good. Additionally, you want the audience to enjoy using your website, and to make sure that it appeals to the right audience. Using CRAP design principles, and having a continuous colour scheme will help improve cohesiveness which will make people happy to be using the website.
    14
  4. Future proofing
    Future proofing the website ensures that the website is kept up to date. This means that outdated content can be deleted, old but still necessary content can be updated, and new information can also be added. Since most users do not know how to do these manually, it is important that there are easy means for any user to be able to perform these functions. Future proofing is important because it makes the website timeless, and always relevant. This means that anyone at any point in time can find some use in accessing it! By using databases to manage the website, store content and make the form option available means that the website will always be able to be used and won’t waste away.

Database Development and Integration

Relevant Implications - Databases (describe)

  1. Privacy
    Internet privacy is the safekeeping of users’ personal data published on the internet, ensuring that there is a high level of privacy and security so that their details are not available for the public or malicious parties to access. This may include things like personal information such as age, email, passwords, location etc, or it could even be photographs of people. When thinking about privacy, an administrator needs to think about two things: firstly, how to reduce the number of personal details need to be given, and secondly, that any data is kept safe. This looks like If personal information is being shared, then users should also have the ability to delete this (e.g. delete their accounts, change their password, hide information like age and location). But generally, things like addresses and phone numbers which are not relevant do not need to be received when signing up for the site. In the case of photographs, permission would need to be obtained from those in the photos. These people should also have the right / ability to take these images down at any point should they no longer consent.
  2. Functionality
    Functionality involves ensuring that the website works effectively and efficiently to be useful to anyone who uses it. In terms of effectiveness, this looks like making sure that all the links are working, and are directing users to the correct pages - this applies not only for links to other pages, or websites, but also for objects like buttons which need to perform the correct function. Functionality efficiency includes making sure that pages do not take a long time to load, and there are no other glitches or bugs on the site. For databases, the databases should be functional, work in the correct way to produce the right output. Examples include ensuring that the correct member is displayed in correspondence with the post they made, or that when accessing the draw, it queries and outputs the correct information (an example where this could go wrong would be trying to find the Prem A draw, but the Junior Prem details are outputted instead). To maintain good functionality, we use database plans which allow us to view entity relationships, and we can also install the sql installer file to make small changes. To improve functionality, testing should always be done to ensure that the database is working, but also testing the outputted material in all situations (e.g. how does creating multiple news posts affect how it shows on the page, does the container need a scroll…).
  3. Sustainability and Future Proofing
    The database should be easily maintained by the administrator, and any edits that need to be made are central to only one area. This isn't true for database tables such as news where other tables like member need to be referenced with a foreign key, or with comments, where two foreign keys - user and news are both referred to. Therefore, if any changes are made to the member table, then all tables that reference this member table with a foreign key will automatically adjust with the changes instead of needing to manually change each member within each table. The key principle with sustainability and future proofing is that the website can be kept up to date so that users in one, five, ten years time have the most relevant information. To an extent, this is contingent on the administration keeping things up to date, but generally, the idea is that the website can be kept up to date easily and quickly. For example, using the edit button to change the draw if something changes, or deleting not so recent news.
  4. End User Considerations
    End-user considerations is the umbrella term for relevant implications such as functionality, usability, aesthetics, accessibility and health & safety because these all relate to the user who will be interacting with the website. However, generally, end-user considerations relate most to usability, ensuring that user’s needs, expectations and requirements of the website are met. The data in the database, and ability to enter, delete and edit data should be queried and displayed in a way that is easy for users to understand and to use. Users should also enjoy the design interface, therefore, it should follow the usability heuristics which includes having an aesthetic and minimalist design. Therefore, styling and forms that are created should be both appealing, but easy to interact with (clear and organised). Additionally, forms on the website should have catches that prevent users from submitting forms with incorrect (e.g. not putting in a valid email address in the sign-up form) or impartial information (e.g. when submitting a post to the upcoming events section or recent news).

            sql = """select news.news_id, news.title, news.subtitle, news.content, news.newsdate, member.firstname
            from news
            join member on news.member_id = member.member_id
            order by news.newsdate desc;
            """

Relevant Implications - Databases (address)

  1. Privacy
    Unfortunately, attention has not been focused on the privacy aspect of this site. However, this is not of huge concern to us at this moment because the databases are unable to be directly accessed by any users of the site. Using the shortcut http:..../data in the search bar will not take users to the data folder. In the case where the builder (myself) or any other admins are able to access the data folder, all passwords are censored out. Additionally, the site is not built with the intention of collecting much personal or valuable data from each of the users as there is no need or use for that. Therefore, any data that is collected by the site should not need to be of any concern to the users. The maximum amount of data that is collected is a password, and email - both of which are not sources of compromising information such as an address, age, school etc would be. We also have not done much about the privacy of persons displayed in our photos. The current photos in the site as of this moment were taken and displayed with permission, and it will be up to the administrators to take down photos wherein the person photographed no longer consents to having their image displayed on the page. Photos that are attached to (eg) an upcoming news item, or past event which are connected to databases have the ability to be removed as part of the "edit" function on these specific databases.
  2. Functionality
    Through the construction of this website, all of the functions have been tested regularly and thoroughly. This means that I checked through all of the button functions, made sure that all of the links were taking the user to the correct page (this was especially important for the draw page because it was just one page and was dependent on the variable it “received”). When I found a glitch or a bug, I would focus on making sure that it was fixed before I continued with any other tasks, this meant that nothing was left forgotten, and it also meant that I wasn’t accidentally making the problem worse if databases were linked. In the development process of my databases, I would perform test queries before moving the code onto my server.py and before using it on my front-end page. The ability to look at my tables through the database table viewer was also particularly useful in ensuring that the correct information was being stored. Generally, data was only kept in one table, and if it was needed somewhere else, then I used foreign keys to reference the correct table. For example, in both schedule and news, they used a foreign key to reference the member table. My comments were a unique case which used TWO foreign keys (news and member). By using the SQL installer file, I was able to make small changes, and feel confident. The db_functions.py also let me reset anything to the base level if I had logged in, or created a lot of news posts which I no longer wanted to remain on the page.
  3. Sustainability and Future Proofing
    It is really easy to add new features due to the relational database design which connects various entities to one another. This means that existing tables don’t need to be adjusted to cater for the addition of a new table. Additionally, by using foreign keys, it means that if another table is referenced (as I mentioned earlier), then the related table will automatically update without needing to do so manually. This means that everything is central to one point, and users don’t need to be making changes in multiple areas which improves the efficiency of the site. Additionally, with the incorporation of create, edit and delete buttons, it is easy for the website to be kept up to date if one chooses to do so. Users are directed to an easy to use form with no hitch. For the creation of the 'draw', I initially planned to have three different tables - grade, round, and finally the draw - however, this was excessive and in order to reduce resources and to make it easier to maintain everything, I condensed everything into one table. In this situation, the grade, round and draw did not have to be separate entities, and could still effectively operate in one table. This made it much easier for a user to make edits, or to add a new draw because it was all in one table rather than three.
  4. End User Considerations
    Although most of the design elements in the site have consideration of viewers who are using different devices (e.g. a mobile phone), I have not necessarily considered these varying screen sizes when it comes to the design and styling of the forms thus far. Whilst they should still be usable, they may not be to the standard that is comfortable for all types of users. However, for laptop users, all of the forms and ability to query and post data is easy and displayed well. They are easy to interact with because the forms clearly direct what is required in a section, and also have extra catches which will prevent a user from submitting too early (not null), or filling in the same information twice (unique). When the user has filled out the form and submitted, it should take them directly to the page where the information has been outputted onto so that they can confirm that their change has been processed correctly. If not, then an error page with an error message will be displayed. I did not initially have this for the draw page, but this is extremely important and now is working fine.

Relevant Implications - SUmmary

  1. Intellectual Property
    Through my website, intellectual property was a relevant implication that needed a lot of consideration. This is because I needed a lot of photos on my website to showcase the talented debaters. To address this, I photographed the majority of the images used on the website, ensuring that I obtained permission from the people captured in them.   2. Accessibility
    Unfortunately, I do not think that accessibility is necessarily maximised for all types of users, Firstly, with the resources available, the coding has primarily been written to work on a laptop or computer, however, I did put in significant efforts to adjust most of the design elements and features for mobile users as well. However, this may mean that some features like entering a post may be difficult for users on mobile devices which is a flaw of this site. However, the website is broadly easy to navigate with a sticky nav bar that allows users to immediately navigate to other pages if they cannot find the information that they need, or if they clicked something wrong. However, accessibility could also be improved for those who have impairments that may affect their online experience. Although I took steps to ensure that font sizing was large enough, that all text colours were appropriate, there is still room for improvement when it comes to ensuring that my website is compatible with text to speech services, better alt tags with my images, skip links, or dyslexia friendly fonts.

  3. Aesthetics
    As my site has a large variety of in the age demographics of potential users, appropriate aesthetic design was an important consideration in my process. Due to the large amounts of information displayed on the site, it was really important the design was kept clean and tidy so as to not overwhelm the users. I used CRAP design principles to help guide my design decisions. For example, each page uses a consistent colour palette of dark grey, light grey, yellow, and white. This colour scheme helped to maintain cohesiveness and clarity, but also gave me opportunities to play with contrasting colours such as yellow text on a dark grey background. Although yellow can sometimes be difficult to read, the difference between the light and dark made the yellow pop out. On the competitions page, I initially had cards which were very close together. This page has quite a lot of information, therefore, I used the principle “proximity” for two purposes: to group relevant information together (e.g. public speaking competitions were kept together with debating competitions separate), and secondly, to space out the cards. The increased space between the cards decreased clutter, ensuring that the information wasn’t clumping together and making it difficult to read.

  4. Future proofing
    One of the common frustrations for users about the old WSU site was that the information was outdated, therefore, this was a very important consideration for my site. This particularly pertains to news about the draws, recent news, and upcoming events. Therefore, these are all places where future proofing is especially important. Other information such as resources has the potential to go out-of-date as styles of debating and adjudicating changes, however, this is relatively low-risk. To combat this, I ensured that most of the resources were aimed at low-level basic ideas of debating, as opposed to trends that may go out. For the draw, recent news, and upcoming events, these are all part of a large database and the administrator has the ability to delete, update, and add new items to keep everything relevant.
